We made it! We're in Tokyo! We only had enough energy to walk around for an hour or two before getting some rest but so far is incredible. And a little overwhelming. I thought; "I live in NYC, and I've been to a lot of big cities. How different could it be?" but it's a bit of a sensory assualt. I'm sure how tired I was from the flight was a factor.

Crossing near the 109 Building in Shibuya

The flight was really easy. We got pretty lucky and had a row of three seats for just the two of us. Which meant our bags could mostly fit under the middle seat and we each had plenty of leg room. In addition to that our seats were only seperated by a closet for the bathroom. So it was easy to get up as much as we needed to to stretch and walk a bit. There was a service gally close to our seats with "help yourself" snacks, ice lemon water, and coffee. I’m not sure if it was excitement or caffeine but I didn’t sleep a wink on the 14 hour flight. I just watched Seinfeld and played Zelda on my Switch. Lastly the food was actually really good. It was a lot of food and wasn’t the very basic snacks I’m used to for vegan options.

When we landed in Tokyo we had zero trouble getting through immigration and customs. Maybe 10 minutes total for the two. After that we exchanged our Japan Rail pass voucher for the actual pass and got tickets for the train to Shibuya. I also got a Suica card with my name on it!

Personalized Suica card

All twelve cars went from Narita to Tokyo station, but at that point, the first six cars went to one destination, while the back six cars continued to the Shinjuku destination.

The view from our Airbnb

We started to get a bit overwhelmed by the crowd, so we stepped outside and I think it was actually more crowded out there. We just grabbed some snacks and headed back to the Airbnb to get some rest before our first full day here!

I kind of just hung around this area for the evening. It reminded me a lot of SoHo in New York. Once it got dark, I got back on the train to visit the Brandenburg Gate.

Brandenburg Gate

The Brandenburg Gate was commissioned by Frederick William II of Prussia and built between 1788 and 1791. It replaced simple guard towers that were the entrance to Berlin. It was used as a political symbol by many of Germany’s leaders including Hitler in the 1930’s. Though it survived World War II it was badly damaged, and during the Cold War in a joint effort between East and West it was restored. It has since undergone more restoration and is now blocked to vehicle traffic. From there I took a short walk to the Reichstag building.

Corner of the Reichstag building

Originally opened in 1894 to house the German Parliament, the building was used as the center of German government until 1933 when it was mostly destroyed in a fire. The Nazi party had taken majority of control of parliament in 1932 and by January of 1933, Adolf Hitler was declared the Chancellor of Germany. On February 27th, 1933 the building caught fire under circumstances still not fully known. Hitler used this fire as a pretext for suppressing political opponents and the next day convinced the Reich President to issue the Reichstag Fire Decree. Taking advantage of emergency powers outlined in the Weimar Constition (signed only 23 years earlier) the decree suspended most of the civil liberties guaranteed to Germans by same document. This was a key step in silencing opponents and political dissidents by granting power to police to arrest outspoken critics and members of opposition parties. Less than a month later, on March 23rd, parliament passed the Enabling Act of 1933, which allowed the cabinet to pass laws without the consent of parliament. This new law effectively gave Hitler dictatorial powers. Soon after, other political parties were outlawed and Germany became a single party state. This is all very heavy, knowing what all happened next, but it’s an important part of history.

I went from here back to Kruezburg to relax and write this post. I’ll try to write up another post tomorrow after I get back. Thanks for reading!

Berlin pt. 2

52º29’15"N 13º23’00"E

21:06 03 Apr 2019

It’s been a few days since I posted but that’s mostly because the thing that pushes me most to add updates are the photos I’ve taken and I haven’t carried my camera the past couple days. The reason I haven’t carried my camera the past few days is this; Germans stare. They stare a lot. In the US we’re raised that staring is rude and in some cases even considered aggressive. So I spent my first couple days here feeling like an alien or like I had an extra limb because people stared at me a lot. It made me really self conscious. Ashley took the initiative that I didn’t to Google it and found that it’s just a German cultural thing. They don’t think it’s rude or invasive to stare they just look at each other more. All this to say that I thought maybe carrying my camera made me stick out as a tourist and I just wanted to blend in. After learning that staring is just something Germans do, it made it much easier to ignore.

A couple other observations about Berlin:
- People are friendly and will quickly switch to English when you look blankly at them. In some places they’ll just first ask "Deutsche oder englisch?"
- "How are you?" Is answered honestly. It’s not just a common pleasantry.
- People here don’t jaywalk. I don’t understand it. There are no cars but they act like there is some sort of force field preventing them from crossing until the sign turns green.
- The vegan food is amazing and it’s everywhere. In New York there are plenty of options, but nearly every food place I walk by here has something vegan on the menu.
- Everyone wears all black. I thought New Yorkers dressed pretty dark, but the majority of people here are wearing black or dark colors head to toe. I’m into it.
- Very few people walk around with headphones in. This really surprised me. In New York and everywhere we went in Japan, most people have headphones in walking around or on public transit. Probably 80% of people are listening to something. Here, I’d say it’s closer to 10% or less.


The past couple days have been mostly spent in museums. I do have some photos but they’re from my phone so they’re not great.

On Sunday I went to Topography of Terror. This is built on the site of the former SS headquarters. This museum shows the timeline of the Nazi rise and then how this location was used to architect and orchestrate the holocaust. It was very moving but sickening to see.

Part of the Berlin Wall that remains near Topography of Terror

A newspaper from the day The Enabling Act was passed making Hitler a dictator

I went from there to Checkpoint Charlie which was the most famous point of crossing the Berlin Wall. I was surprised by how much it felt like a theme park attraction now.

Checkpoint Charlie

On Monday I visited the Jewish Museum which is mostly a memorial for the holocaust. It’s very moving. The museum itself is built to be kind of maze like with confusing perspectives. Hallways look long and the angles are jagged. I think this is meant to make you feel confused and like you don’t know where to turn, much like the Jews of Europe in the 1930’s and 1940’s. There are two very impactful pieces in this part of the museum. The first I went into was called Holocaust Tower. You enter the base of a three story tower with only a small sliver of light at the top. It’s like being at the bottom of a well. There were some others guests in there when I walked in but after they left and I was alone, it quickly became overwhelming. I’m sure this was the architect’s intention. Another large work that’s very moving is called Memory Void.

Memory Void floor

Memory Void

This is a large open section where the floor is covered with hundreds of metal plates that have a haunting face of anguish on them. I image each plate represents thousands of lives lost during the holocaust.

After the Jewish museum I visited Berliniche Galerie, a modern art museum one block over. This museum was pretty small but it had a lot of really cool pieces.

Berliniche Galerie

One thing I thought was really interesting was how they had small 3D renderings of the more prominent pieces. This small versions are for the vision impaired guests. They’re able to feel the art.

Small 3D recreation of the art seen on the left

After all that I went to an all vegan supermarket appropriately called Veganz.

Veganz

All the stuff I got at Veganz

Tuesday I went to what’s called Museum Island. It’s a small island in the Spree river that has 5 large museums. I visited two of the five. The first is called Pergamonmuseum, an ancient history museum. The first thing you see here is The Eighth Gate of Babylon, called the Ishtar Gate.

A small section of the Ishtar Gate

A miniature recreation of the Ishtar gate how it was originally used

The gate was originally built by King Nebuchadnezzar II of Babylon in 575 BCE. It was excavated from present day Hillah, Iraq, 53 miles south of Baghdad, from 1902 to 1914, then moved brick by brick to Berlin and completed in 1930. The gate stands 46 ft tall and 100 ft wide. It’s really impressive to stand in front of something 2,600 years old.

Another piece that struck me in the museum was this one.

Authentication stamp

The small white cylinder on the left is a stamp. It rolls out as seen on the red clay to the right of it. This was used as a signature to authenticate documents and contracts. The really mind blowing thing about it is that it’s from around 2,500 BCE. 4,500 years ago, a person made this as a way to prevent forgery.

Today was pretty lazy. I walked over to a small coffee shop close to my Airbnb called Okay Cafe. I had a great breakfast of overnight oats and fruit and just sat and wrote in my travel journal most of the morning. I also tried to plan out what I’ll do tomorrow. The cafe did have a very cute dog that would walk in every 20 minutes or so to check for crumbs under each table and then move a long.

Vacuum cleaner pup

From there I went to the East Side Gallery. A series of paintings done on the east side of the Berlin Wall. Frustratingly though I only took a couple pictures of the West side of that wall which was the same kind of street art you see all over Berlin.

The west side of the east side gallery
